## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The United States battery manufacturing ecosystem has undergone unprecedented expansion from 2021-2025, driven primarily by the Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) and broader electrification trends. This report documents:

- **30+ Major Gigafactories** (>1 GWh capacity) - Total announced capacity: ~800+ GWh by 2027-2030
- **15+ Component Manufacturing Plants** (cathode, anode, separator, electrolyte production)
- **10+ Battery Recycling Facilities** across 8 states
- **4 Primary Regional Clusters**: Southeast Automotive Corridor, Midwest Battery Belt, Nevada Lithium Complex, Emerging Regions

**Key Finding:** The "Battery Belt" stretching from Michigan through Tennessee to Georgia represents approximately 450+ GWh of announced capacity across 15+ facilities, making it the dominant manufacturing corridor in North America.

## SECTION 4: REGIONAL CLUSTER ANALYSIS

### 4.1 SOUTHEAST AUTOMOTIVE CORRIDOR (GA, TN, NC, SC, KY, AL)

**Total Announced Capacity:** ~500+ GWh
**Number of Gigafactories:** 15+
**Employment Impact:** 20,000+ direct manufacturing jobs

#### Key Characteristics:
1. **OEM Integration:** Co-located with Toyota, GM, Ford, Hyundai, Nissan, Mercedes, BMW assembly plants
2. **Supply Chain Density:** Tier 1/2 automotive suppliers provide immediate ecosystem
3. **Logistics Advantage:** Port of Savannah, Port of Charleston; I-75/I-40/I-85 interstate corridors
4. **Policy Support:** Tennessee ($884M incentive for Ford BlueOval), Georgia ($1.8B for Hyundai-LG), Kentucky competitive packages
5. **Workforce:** Established automotive workforce; technical colleges aligned with battery manufacturing training

#### Dominant Players:
- **LG Energy Solution:** Georgia (Hyundai JV), Tennessee (GM JV), Ohio (Honda JV) - integrated vertical strategy
- **Ford-SK On (BlueOval SK):** Kentucky (86 GWh total), Tennessee (43 GWh)
- **GM-LG (Ultium Cells):** Tennessee (50 GWh)
- **Toyota:** North Carolina (30 GWh)

#### Cluster Anchors:
- **Georgia:** Port access + Hyundai/Kia ecosystem
- **Tennessee:** Ford/GM assembly + central logistics position
- **Kentucky:** BlueOval SK largest single-site investment ($5.8B)
- **Carolinas:** Toyota (NC) + AESC/Redwood (SC) + emerging recycling hub

**Competitive Advantage:** Fastest path to market for OEMs due to proximity; reduces logistics costs and supply chain complexity.

### 4.3 NEVADA LITHIUM COMPLEX

**Total Announced Capacity:** ~120 GWh manufacturing + materials ecosystem
**Number of Facilities:** 4 major (Tesla, Lyten, Panasonic, Redwood Materials)
**Employment Impact:** 10,000+ (incl. Tesla Gigafactory expansion)

#### Key Characteristics:
1. **Vertical Integration:** Mine-to-battery ecosystem
   - Thacker Pass lithium mine (GM-Lithium Americas JV, $650M)
   - Redwood Materials recycling/cathode production
   - Tesla/Panasonic cell manufacturing
   - Energy storage deployment (Tesla Megapack)
2. **Technology Diversity:**
   - Lithium-ion (Tesla/Panasonic)
   - LFP (Tesla)
   - Lithium-sulfur (Lyten - first in world)
3. **Tax/Regulatory Environment:** No corporate income tax; favorable environmental permitting
4. **Renewable Energy:** Abundant solar/geothermal for battery production
5. **Logistics:** Central West Coast position; rail to CA markets

#### Unique Strengths:
- **Only state with full lithium lifecycle** (mining → manufacturing → recycling)
- **Innovation Hub:** Lyten lithium-sulfur technology represents next-gen chemistry
- **Tesla Anchor:** Gigafactory Nevada sets ecosystem standards; Panasonic co-location proven model

**Competitive Advantage:** Lowest carbon intensity batteries in U.S. due to renewable energy integration; domestic lithium supply reduces geopolitical risk.

## SECTION 7: CRITICAL ANALYSIS & INSIGHTS

### 7.1 SUPPLY CHAIN GAPS & VULNERABILITIES

#### Materials Gaps:
1. **Lithium Supply:**
   - Only 1 major domestic mine (Thacker Pass, NV - not yet operational)
   - 80%+ lithium still imported (Chile, Australia, China)
   - Mitigation: Recycling targets 30% supply by 2030 (Redwood, Ascend, Li-Cycle)

2. **Cathode Materials:**
   - Limited domestic CAM capacity vs. cell production
   - China dominates global cathode production (60%+ market share)
   - Announced U.S. capacity: ~150K tonnes/year by 2027 vs. 800+ GWh cell demand
   - **Gap:** Insufficient to support full domestic supply chain

3. **Separator Supply:**
   - ENTEK Terre Haute addresses gap (3.2B sq m = 3.5M EVs/year)
   - Still import-dependent until 2027+ full ramp
   - No domestic competitors at scale (Asahi Kasei, SK IE Technology dominate globally)

4. **Electrolyte Production:**
   - **Critical Gap:** Minimal domestic production capacity
   - Dominated by Korean/Japanese/Chinese suppliers
   - No major U.S. announcements for large-scale electrolyte plants

#### Geographic Concentration Risk:
- **Southeast cluster:** 50%+ of U.S. capacity in 6 states
- **Natural disaster risk:** Hurricane exposure (SC, NC, GA coastal facilities)
- **Labor market risk:** Competition for skilled workforce across 15+ facilities in region

---

### 7.2 TECHNOLOGY TRENDS & FACILITY ADAPTABILITY

#### Chemistry Shifts:
1. **LFP Adoption:**
   - LGES Queen Creek (16 GWh LFP for ESS)
   - Tesla Sparks LFP plant (10 GWh)
   - ICL St. Louis LFP cathode plant
   - **Trend:** ESS + entry-level EVs driving LFP demand

2. **Next-Gen Chemistries:**
   - Lyten lithium-sulfur (10 GWh, Reno) - 40% higher energy density
   - Form Energy iron-air (100-hour storage) - non-lithium
   - **Risk:** Facilities locked into NMC chemistry may require expensive retooling

3. **Solid-State Timeline:**
   - QuantumScape San Jose (pilot/pre-pilot only)
   - No announced gigafactory-scale solid-state facilities in U.S.
   - **Implication:** 2027-2030+ before commercial viability

### 7.3 COMPETITIVE DYNAMICS

#### U.S. vs. Global Capacity:
- **China:** 1,200+ GWh capacity by 2025 (CATL, BYD, CALB)

- **U.S.:** 800+ GWh announced by 2030
- **Gap:** U.S. still 2-3 years behind China in commercial ramp

- **U.S. cost structure:** $100-120/kWh (cell level, 2024)

- **IRA impact:** $45/kWh tax credit narrows gap to competitiveness

## APPENDIX B: GLOSSARY

- **GWh (Gigawatt-hour):** Unit of battery capacity; 1 GWh = ~12,500 EV battery packs (80 kWh each)
- **Black Mass:** Shredded battery material containing lithium, nickel, cobalt, manganese (recycling feedstock)
- **CAM (Cathode Active Material):** Lithium compound forming cathode; 40% of cell cost
- **pCAM (Precursor CAM):** Intermediate material before final cathode synthesis
- **NMC:** Nickel-Manganese-Cobalt cathode chemistry (high energy density)
- **LFP:** Lithium Iron Phosphate cathode chemistry (lower cost, safer, longer cycle life)
- **Wet-Process Separator:** Battery separator manufactured via phase inversion (vs. dry-process); higher performance
- **Spoke & Hub Model:** Li-Cycle's system - spokes (regional shredding) feed hubs (centralized chemical processing)
- **IRA (Inflation Reduction Act):** 2022 U.S. law providing $369B for clean energy, including battery tax credits
- **BlueOval:** Ford's EV sub-brand; BlueOval SK = Ford-SK On joint venture
- **Ultium:** GM's EV platform and battery brand; Ultium Cells = GM-LG JV

## REPORT CONCLUSION

The United States has made extraordinary progress in building domestic battery manufacturing capacity since 2021, with announced capacity exceeding 800 GWh by 2030 - a 20x increase from 2021 levels. The Southeast Automotive Corridor and Midwest Battery Belt have emerged as dominant manufacturing clusters, driven by OEM proximity, policy support, and established automotive ecosystems.

However, significant challenges remain:
1. **Supply Chain Gaps:** Cathode, anode, electrolyte, and separator capacity lags cell production
2. **Import Dependence:** Lithium, graphite, and processed materials still 70%+ imported
3. **Execution Risk:** 30-50% of announced capacity may face delays or cancellations due to demand uncertainty
4. **Cost Competitiveness:** U.S. manufacturing costs 20-30% higher than China without IRA subsidies

**The path forward requires:**
- Aggressive upstream investment (materials processing, not just cells)
- Recycling infrastructure to close the loop (500K+ EVs/year capacity needed by 2030)
- Workforce development at unprecedented scale (100K+ skilled workers)
- Policy stability to de-risk long-term capital commitments

With disciplined execution and sustained policy support, the U.S. can achieve 50-60% battery supply chain localization by 2030, positioning North America as a globally competitive battery manufacturing hub.
