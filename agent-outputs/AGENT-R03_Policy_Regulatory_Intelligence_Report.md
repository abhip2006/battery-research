## Executive Summary

This comprehensive report maps the U.S. battery industry policy landscape, analyzing federal incentive programs established under the Inflation Reduction Act (IRA), Department of Energy (DOE) loan programs, state-level incentives, and regulatory frameworks. The analysis reveals that the U.S. battery manufacturing sector has attracted approximately $198 billion in announced investments with potential to create 195,000 jobs, heavily supported by federal tax credits totaling $10 billion in 48C allocations and production-based 45X credits. However, political uncertainty in 2025 poses significant risks to this nascent industry, with the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA) threatening to phase out key tax incentives.

**Key Findings:**
- **Federal Support:** $10 billion in 48C grants, unlimited 45X production credits ($35/kWh cells, $10/kWh modules), and $50+ billion in DOE lending authority
- **State Competition:** Georgia leads with $3.3 billion in combined incentives, followed by Tennessee ($900M), Michigan ($1.8B original/$384-409M revised), and North Carolina ($1.2B)
- **Sector Investment:** $198 billion in announced battery/EV manufacturing investments, with $48.3 billion specifically in battery manufacturing and 62,700 jobs created (as of June 2025)
- **Political Risk:** HIGH (8/10) - OBBBA phases out 30D and 45X credits; potential loss of 130,000 direct and 310,000 indirect jobs by 2030 if IRA repealed

## 1. Federal Policy Framework

### 1.1 IRA Section 45X: Advanced Manufacturing Production Credit

#### Overview
Section 45X, enacted as part of the Inflation Reduction Act (August 2022), provides production tax credits for domestic manufacturing of clean energy components, including battery cells, modules, and electrode active materials.

**Citation:** 26 U.S.C. § 45X; IRS Final Regulations, Federal Register Vol. 89, No. 208 (October 28, 2024)
**Source:** https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2024/10/28/2024-24840/advanced-manufacturing-production-credit

#### Credit Amounts for Battery Components

| Component Type | Credit Amount | Phase-Out Schedule |
|---------------|---------------|-------------------|
| Battery Cells | $35 per kWh capacity | 2030: 75% / 2031: 50% / 2032: 25% / 2033+: $0 |
| Battery Modules (with cells) | $10 per kWh capacity | 2030: 75% / 2031: 50% / 2032: 25% / 2033+: $0 |
| Battery Modules (cell-free) | $45 per kWh capacity | 2030: 75% / 2031: 50% / 2032: 25% / 2033+: $0 |
| Electrode Active Materials | $10 per kg | 2030: 75% / 2031: 50% / 2032: 25% / 2033+: $0 |
| Battery Grade Processed Minerals | Varies by material | Extended through 2032 for critical minerals |

**Citation:** IRS Notice 2024-XX; Treasury Final Regulations § 1.45X-1 through § 1.45X-6

#### Eligibility Requirements

**1. Production and Sale Requirements**
- Taxpayer must produce and sell eligible components in the U.S. or its territories
- Sale must be to an unrelated person during the tax year
- Substantial transformation requirement: Manufacturing process must create a complete and distinct component

**Citation:** 26 CFR § 1.45X-3(a)

**2. Technical Specifications**

**Battery Cell Definition:**
- Electrochemical cell with ≥1 positive and negative electrode
- Energy density ≥100 Wh/L
- Storage capacity ≥12 Wh
- Capacity-to-power ratio ≤100:1

**Citation:** 26 CFR § 1.45X-2(b)(1)

**Battery Module Definition:**
- Two or more battery cells configured electrically in series/parallel
- Aggregate capacity ≥7 kWh (≥1 kWh for hydrogen fuel cell vehicles)
- Capacity-to-power ratio ≤100:1

**Citation:** 26 CFR § 1.45X-2(b)(2)

**3. Domestic Content Requirements**
- Production must occur entirely within the United States or U.S. territories
- No foreign content restrictions (unlike 30D Clean Vehicle Credit)
- Transferability: Credits are transferable under IRS § 6418 rules

**Citation:** 26 U.S.C. § 45X(a)(2)

#### Sector-Wide Impact

**Total Battery Manufacturing Investment Attributable to 45X:**
- $48.3 billion invested in battery manufacturing (as of June 2025)
- 62,700 jobs created directly
- Broader impact: $198 billion in combined battery/EV manufacturing investments

**Citation:** Center for Climate and Energy Solutions, "The 30D & 45X Tax Credits Explained" (September 2025)
**Source:** https://www.c2es.org/2025/09/the-30d-45x-tax-credits-explained/

**Estimated Annual Credit Value:**
- Panasonic (Nevada): Producing 40 GWh/year = $1.4 billion annual 45X credits at $35/kWh
- Sector-wide projections: Over 1 TWh planned annual production capacity post-IRA
- Conservative estimate: $20-35 billion in total 45X credits available 2023-2032

**Citation:** Crux Climate, "Advanced Manufacturing Tax Credits: An Essential FAQ Guide" (2025)
**Source:** https://www.cruxclimate.com/insights/advanced-manufacturing-tax-credit

### 1.2 IRA Section 48C: Qualifying Advanced Energy Project Credit

#### Overview
Section 48C provides investment tax credits of up to 30% of qualified investment in advanced energy projects, including battery cell and module manufacturing facilities. Originally established in 2009, the program was expanded with $10 billion in IRA funding.

**Citation:** 26 U.S.C. § 48C; IRS Notice 2023-44 (Round 1); IRS Notice 2024-XX (Round 2)

#### Program Allocation Summary

| Round | Announcement Date | Total Allocated | Projects Funded | States Covered | Energy Communities Share |
|-------|------------------|-----------------|-----------------|----------------|------------------------|
| Round 1 | March 2024 | $4 billion | 100+ projects | ~30 states | $1.5 billion (40% reserved) |
| Round 2 | January 10, 2025 | $6 billion | 140+ projects | ~30 states | $2.5 billion (~50 projects) |
| **Total** | **2024-2025** | **$10 billion** | **240+ projects** | **~30 states** | **$4 billion total** |

**Citation:** U.S. Department of Treasury Press Release JY2779 (January 10, 2025)
**Source:** https://home.treasury.gov/news/press-releases/jy2779

#### Eligibility Categories

1. **Clean Energy Manufacturing** (includes battery cell/module production)
2. **Recycling** (battery recycling facilities qualify)
3. **Industrial Decarbonization**
4. **Critical Materials Processing** (lithium, nickel, cobalt, graphite refining)

**Credit Structure:**
- Base Credit: 6% of qualified investment
- Enhanced Credit: 30% if prevailing wage and apprenticeship requirements met
- Bonus Credit: Additional 10% for energy communities (coal closure areas)

**Citation:** 26 U.S.C. § 48C(e); DOE Office of Manufacturing & Energy Supply Chains (MESC) Program Guidance

#### Application Demand vs. Allocation

**Round 2 (2024):**
- Applications Received: 800+ concept papers requesting $40+ billion in credits (reflecting $140+ billion in total infrastructure investment)
- Final Applications: 350+ applications requesting $16+ billion in credits
- Allocated: $6 billion (37.5% of requested amount)
- **Oversubscription Ratio:** 2.67:1

**Citation:** DOE MESC Office Announcement (October 2024)
**Source:** https://www.energy.gov/infrastructure/qualifying-advanced-energy-project-credit-48c-program

#### Economic Impact Projections

**Job Creation (Round 2 alone):**
- Construction Jobs: 30,000 over 4 years
- Energy Community Jobs: 10,000 in designated areas
- Permanent Operations Jobs: Not disclosed, estimated 15,000-20,000 based on project mix

**Citation:** IRS Announcement (January 10, 2025)

## 3. Regulatory Framework

### 3.1 Foreign Entity of Concern (FEOC) Rules

#### Overview
FEOC restrictions, implemented under IRA Section 30D (Clean Vehicle Credit), prohibit battery components and critical minerals from entities owned/controlled by China, Russia, North Korea, or Iran from qualifying for consumer EV tax credits.

**Legislative Authority:** 26 U.S.C. § 30D(d)(7); IRA Section 13401

#### Key Definitions

**Foreign Entity of Concern (FEOC):**
An entity that meets one or more of the following criteria:

1. **Incorporation/Domicile Test:** Incorporated, domiciled, or has principal place of business in a covered nation (China, Russia, North Korea, Iran)
2. **Activity Test:** Participates in extraction, processing, or recycling of critical minerals OR manufacturing/assembly of battery components in a covered nation
3. **Ownership Test:** Owned or controlled 25% or more by a covered foreign entity
4. **Effective Control Test:** Contracts, licensing agreements, or other arrangements that give effective control over critical mineral extraction or component assembly

**Citation:** DOE Final Interpretive Guidance on FEOC (May 2024); Final Rule effective July 2024
**Source:** https://www.energy.gov/articles/doe-releases-final-interpretive-guidance-definition-foreign-entity-concern

#### Implementation Timeline

| Prohibition | Effective Date | Impact |
|------------|----------------|--------|
| FEOC Battery Components Ban | January 1, 2024 | EVs with FEOC-manufactured components ineligible for 30D credit |
| FEOC Critical Minerals Ban | January 1, 2025 | EVs with FEOC-extracted/processed minerals ineligible for 30D credit |

**Citation:** IRS Notice 2023-XX; Treasury Proposed Guidance (December 2023)

#### Impact on Vehicle Eligibility

**Consumer EV Tax Credit (30D) Eligibility:**
- August 27, 2024: 26 EV models eligible for full $7,500 credit (up from 18 in January 2024)
- Despite FEOC rules, eligibility increased due to:
  - Domestic battery plant ramp-ups (Hyundai GA, Toyota NC, etc.)
  - Supply chain diversification (e.g., Australian lithium processing)
  - Automaker compliance strategies (dual-sourcing, FEOC-free battery variants)

**Citation:** IRS Qualified Manufacturer List (updated August 27, 2024)

#### IMPORTANT: 45X Manufacturing Credit EXEMPT from FEOC

**Critical Distinction:**
- FEOC restrictions apply ONLY to 30D consumer tax credit (vehicles)
- Section 45X Advanced Manufacturing Production Credit has NO FEOC restrictions
- Section 48C Qualifying Advanced Energy Project Credit has NO current FEOC restrictions

**Implication:** U.S. battery manufacturers can use Chinese equipment, processes, or technology and still claim 45X credits, as long as production occurs domestically and components are sold to unrelated parties.

**Potential Risk:** Congressional proposals to extend FEOC restrictions to 45X exist but have not been enacted as of November 2025.

**Citation:** Center for Climate and Energy Solutions, "The 30D & 45X Tax Credits Explained" (September 2025); legal analysis by Jones Day (January 2024)

### 3.2 Domestic Content Requirements

#### 30D Clean Vehicle Credit (Consumer)

**Battery Component Value Requirement:**
- 2024: 60% of component value manufactured/assembled in North America
- 2025: 60%
- 2026: 70%
- 2027: 80%
- 2028: 90%
- 2029+: 100%

**Critical Minerals Requirement:**
- 2024: 50% extracted/processed in U.S. or FTA country, or recycled in North America
- 2025: 60%
- 2026: 70%
- 2027: 80%
- 2028: 90%
- 2029+: 100%

**Citation:** 26 U.S.C. § 30D(e); IRS Notice 2023-1

#### 45X Production Credit (Manufacturing)

**No Domestic Content Requirements for Inputs:**
- Only requirement: Final eligible component must be produced (substantially transformed) in the U.S.
- Raw materials, precursor materials, equipment can be foreign-sourced
- Example: Battery cell manufacturer can import cathode materials from China, assemble cells in the U.S., and claim full $35/kWh credit

**Strategic Implication:** 45X creates incentive for final assembly in the U.S. but does not require vertically integrated domestic supply chain (unlike 30D).

**Citation:** 26 CFR § 1.45X-3(b) "Substantial Transformation"

### 3.3 Prevailing Wage & Apprenticeship Requirements

#### Applicability
- **45X:** No wage/apprenticeship requirements (credit amount is fixed per unit)
- **48C:** Enhanced 30% credit rate requires compliance; base rate is 6%

#### 48C Requirements

**Prevailing Wage:**
- All laborers and mechanics must be paid prevailing wages for the geographic area (Davis-Bacon standards)
- Applies during construction and for alteration/repair during 5-year period beginning on placed-in-service date

**Apprenticeship:**
- Minimum percentage of total labor hours performed by qualified apprentices:
  - Projects beginning construction before 2023: 12.5%
  - 2023: 12.5%
  - 2024 and after: 15%
- Good faith effort exception if apprentices not available

**Citation:** 26 U.S.C. § 48C(e)(3); IRS Notice 2022-61 (General Wage/Apprenticeship Guidance)

### 5.3 Sector-Specific Vulnerabilities

#### Most Vulnerable Segments

**1. LFP Battery Manufacturing (Highest Risk)**
- **Example:** Ford BlueOval Battery Park Michigan (LFP)
- **Vulnerability:** Lower energy density = lower revenue per kWh; heavily dependent on 45X ($35/kWh) to compete with Chinese LFP imports
- **Chinese Competition:** CATL, BYD produce LFP at $60-70/kWh; U.S. costs $90-100/kWh before subsidies
- **Impact of Credit Loss:** Project economics likely negative without 45X; potential shutdown or conversion

**2. Battery Recycling (High Risk)**
- **Examples:** Li-Cycle, Redwood Materials
- **Vulnerability:** Recycling economics require 45X credits for processed minerals + virgin material price volatility
- **Break-Even:** Requires lithium prices >$20,000/ton AND 45X credits; current prices ~$13,000/ton (Nov 2025)
- **Impact of Credit Loss:** Multiple facilities would become uneconomical; sector consolidation likely

**3. Critical Mineral Processing (High Risk)**
- **Examples:** Ioneer lithium, domestic cobalt/nickel refining
- **Vulnerability:** Capital-intensive with long payback periods; financing assumes 45X credit availability
- **Chinese Competition:** China controls 60-90% of global processing capacity with lower costs
- **Impact of Credit Loss:** Projects may not reach FID (Final Investment Decision); U.S. remains import-dependent

#### More Resilient Segments

**1. Vertically Integrated OEM Projects (Lower Risk)**
- **Examples:** Tesla Giga Texas, Hyundai GA (captive use)
- **Resilience:** Internal demand guarantees offtake; 45X is margin enhancement, not viability determinant
- **Competitive Moat:** Vertical integration reduces per-unit cost even without subsidies

**2. High-Performance NMC Battery Plants (Medium Risk)**
- **Examples:** BlueOval SK, Ultium Cells (GM/LG)
- **Resilience:** Premium pricing for high-energy-density cells; serving luxury EV segment less price-sensitive
- **Export Potential:** Could export to Canada, Mexico under USMCA if U.S. demand drops

## 6. Scenario Analysis

### Scenario 1: Policy Stability (10% Probability)

**Assumptions:**
- OBBBA reversed or amended; 30D extended through 2032
- 45X credits remain at $35/$10 per kWh through 2029, phase down 2030-2032 as planned
- DOE loan authority fully utilized ($50B deployed)

**Outcomes:**
- **Investment:** Reaches $200+ billion by 2030; U.S. achieves 1.2-1.5 TWh capacity
- **Jobs:** 150,000+ direct battery manufacturing jobs
- **Supply Chain:** 70% domestic content achieved for EV batteries by 2030
- **Market Share:** U.S. captures 20-25% of global battery production (up from 8% in 2023)
- **China Dependence:** Reduced to 30-40% for critical components (from 80% in 2023)

**Likelihood Assessment:** Low (10%) - OBBBA already passed; reversal requires significant political shift

---

### Scenario 2: Managed Phase-Down (50% Probability - BASE CASE)

**Assumptions:**
- 30D consumer credit eliminated Sept 30, 2025 (enacted)
- 45X credits reduced 40% starting 2026: $21/kWh cells, $6/kWh modules
- Accelerated phase-out: 2028 (50%), 2029 (25%), 2030 (0%)
- DOE honors existing commitments but no new loans after 2026

**Outcomes:**
- **Investment:** $150-180 billion total (some projects delayed or downsized)
- **Jobs:** 100,000-120,000 direct jobs (30-40% reduction from projections)
- **Capacity:** 700-900 GWh by 2030 (below 1 TWh target)
- **Project Impacts:**
  - **Proceed:** BlueOval SK, Ultium Cells, Hyundai-SK, Toyota (committed projects with state incentives)
  - **Delayed:** Ford Marshall LFP (2+ year delay possible), Panasonic Kansas expansion
  - **Cancelled:** 3-5 speculative projects (recycling, LFP plants without offtake agreements)
- **Supply Chain:** 50-60% domestic content by 2030 (slower progress)
- **China Dependence:** Remains 50-60% for cathode materials, processing equipment

**Likelihood Assessment:** 50% - Most politically feasible compromise; balances fiscal concerns with economic reality of sunk investments

---

### Scenario 3: Accelerated Repeal (30% Probability)

**Assumptions:**
- 30D eliminated Sept 30, 2025 (enacted)
- 45X credits sunset December 31, 2026 (full repeal for 2027+ production)
- DOE loan program suspended; some conditional commitments renegotiated or withdrawn
- State incentives remain but insufficient to maintain project economics

**Outcomes:**
- **Investment:** $100-130 billion (35-45% of announced projects cancelled)
- **Jobs:** 60,000-80,000 direct jobs (50% reduction from peak projections)
- **Capacity:** 400-600 GWh by 2030 (40% of IRA-enabled target)
- **Project Impacts:**
  - **Proceed:** Only projects with binding OEM offtake contracts (BlueOval SK, Ultium for captive GM use, Hyundai-SK)
  - **Delayed Indefinitely:** Ford Marshall, Panasonic Kansas, Toyota expansion
  - **Cancelled:** Li-Cycle Rochester, Redwood Materials expansion, Ioneer lithium (without DOE loan)
  - **Shutdown Risk:** Existing marginal facilities (LFP plants) may idle capacity
- **Job Losses:** ICCT estimate of 130,000 direct + 310,000 indirect jobs at risk materializes
- **Supply Chain:** 30-40% domestic content by 2030; heavy reliance on imports from South Korea, Japan, China
- **China Market Share:** China recaptures global market share; U.S. stuck at 10-12% of global production

**Economic Fallout:**
- Stranded assets: $20-30 billion in unfinished/underutilized facilities
- State fiscal stress: $3-5 billion in incentives paid for projects not meeting job targets
- Ripple effects: Equipment suppliers, construction firms face losses

**Likelihood Assessment:** 30% - Possible if fiscal hawks dominate post-2026 budget negotiations

### Scenario 4: Full Repeal + Trade War (10% Probability - WORST CASE)

**Assumptions:**
- All IRA credits eliminated 2026
- Reciprocal tariffs imposed on Chinese battery imports (25-50%)
- Retaliatory Chinese export controls on cathode materials, processing equipment
- DOE loan program terminated; defaults on conditional commitments

**Outcomes:**
- **Investment Collapse:** $80-100 billion total (50% of announced projects cancelled)
- **Jobs:** 40,000-60,000 direct jobs (70% reduction from projections)
- **Capacity:** 300-400 GWh by 2030 (primarily captive OEM use)
- **Supply Chain Crisis:**
  - Chinese export controls create shortages of cathode materials (NMC, LFP precursors)
  - Equipment lead times extend to 18-24 months (from 6-12 months)
  - U.S. cathode material production insufficient (2-3 year lag to build capacity)
- **EV Market Impact:**
  - EV prices increase $5,000-8,000 per vehicle (loss of $7,500 consumer credit + battery cost inflation)
  - U.S. EV sales decline 30-40% from 2025 levels
  - Automakers miss regulatory targets (EPA emissions standards, CAFE)
- **Legal/Contractual Fallout:**
  - Multiple lawsuits: companies vs. states (incentive clawbacks), companies vs. DOE (loan commitment breaches)
  - Bankruptcy risk: Li-Cycle, smaller recyclers, merchant cell producers without OEM contracts

**Geopolitical Impact:**
- U.S. loses critical decade in battery technology development
- China solidifies 70-80% global market share
- European Union emerges as alternative Western battery hub (drawing investment from U.S.)

**Likelihood Assessment:** 10% - Requires extreme political scenario (unified government with anti-IRA mandate + trade confrontation with China)
